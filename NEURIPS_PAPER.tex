\begin{abstract}
Standard Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models condition each action on the
current visual observation and a language instruction, discarding two
informative signals: \emph{(i)} what action the agent attempted last step,
and \emph{(ii)} whether that action worked.
We present \modelname\ (\modelnamefull), a five-stream multimodal robot
policy that re-injects both signals as \emph{language tokens} before fusion.
At each timestep, the previous action is looked up in a pre-trained CLIP
vocabulary and encoded as a semantic token~\Lact, while a concurrently
generated natural-language description of the observed outcome
(``\emph{As a result, I got closer to the goal}'') is CLIP-encoded as a
second consequence token~\Lcon.
A rolling window of $(a_t, r_t)$ pairs forms a fourth stream of history
tokens.
All five streams share a frozen CLIP ViT-B/32 backbone (vision and three
language streams) and are fused by a six-layer LLaMA-style Transformer
(RMSNorm, RoPE, SwiGLU) with ViLT modality-type embeddings.
An auxiliary reward-weighted InfoNCE loss encourages the instruction
embedding to be semantically aligned with both action and consequence
embeddings for successful steps.
Two-phase training (behavioural cloning followed by KL-regularised REINFORCE)
provides stable initialisation and online improvement.
With only $\approx$4.2M trainable parameters out of $\approx$128M total,
\modelname\ trains on a single consumer GPU.
Ablation studies on BabyAI and Meta-World validate the contribution of each
stream: consequence language feedback alone improves task success rate by
$+$X\% over the action-only feedback baseline (Ablation~F), and dual
language feedback outperforms numerical-only history conditioning
(Ablation~A) by $+$Y\% (results to be filled upon experiment completion).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:Introduction}

Embodied AI agents must make long sequences of decisions in which earlier
failures should change subsequent strategy.
Modern Vision-Language-Action models such as
RT-2~\citep{brohan2023rt2} (55B parameters),
Octo~\citep{octo2024} (93M parameters), and
OpenVLA~\citep{kim2024openvla} treat each action prediction as a
\emph{stateless} function of the current frame and instruction.
The reward received after the previous action — the primary signal in any
reinforcement learning formulation — is absent at inference time.

Two concrete failure modes follow from this design.
\textbf{No recovery signal}: when a robot repeats a failing grasp, the
policy cannot distinguish a first attempt from a fifth failed one.
\textbf{No consequence grounding}: even though RL training maximises
reward, the learned policy discards the reward at test time, making the
RL training signal invisible to the inference-time reasoning process.

\paragraph{Our approach: language as the feedback channel.}
Rather than representing history as raw numerical tuples $(a_t, r_t)$, we
map them to \emph{natural language} — the same modality CLIP was pretrained
on — and re-encode them through the shared frozen CLIP text encoder.
This exploits CLIP's existing semantic structure: the phrase
``\emph{The action was successful}'' is already embedded close to
``\emph{pick up the object}'' in CLIP's 512-dim text space, requiring only
a trainable 512$\to$256 projection to integrate with the fusion backbone.

We make two language feedback streams:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Stream~3a — Action Language} (\Lact): the previous action
        index $a_{t-1}$ is mapped through a fixed vocabulary (``\emph{I
        moved the robot arm to the left}'') and CLIP-encoded.
        A learned reward gate $\sigma(\text{MLP}(r_{t-1}))\in(0,1)$
        scales the token — failed actions contribute proportionally less.
  \item \textbf{Stream~3b — Consequence Language} (\Lcon, \textbf{new}):
        a \texttt{verbalize\_consequence()} function maps the scalar reward
        and the signed distance-to-goal delta $\Delta d_{t-1}$ to a
        natural-language outcome string, CLIP-encoded into an independent
        256-dim token.
        This token captures \emph{what happened}, orthogonal to the action
        description of \emph{what was attempted}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{\modelname}: a five-stream multimodal VLA with dual language
        feedback (action + consequence) fused by a LLaMA-style causal
        Transformer with ViLT modality-type embeddings.
  \item \textbf{Consequence language encoding}: a lightweight
        \texttt{verbalize\_consequence()} function that translates scalar
        reward and state-change signals into CLIP-compatible natural language
        at inference time, enabling zero-shot semantic integration with no
        additional pretraining.
  \item \textbf{Reward-weighted InfoNCE alignment loss}: an auxiliary
        contrastive objective that teaches the fusion space that successful
        actions and their consequences should be semantically close to the
        task instruction.
  \item \textbf{Full experimental infrastructure}: MetaWorld and BabyAI
        environment wrappers with correct \texttt{dist\_delta} extraction,
        multi-seed evaluation, and an automated ablation runner covering
        eight model variants.
  \item \textbf{Architectural analysis}: we document the CLS-causal-mask
        isolation bug from the original codebase and its fix.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:RelatedWork}

\paragraph{Vision-Language-Action Models.}
RT-2~\citep{brohan2023rt2} co-fine-tunes a 55B VLM to output tokenised
robot actions, achieving strong generalisation but requiring TPU-scale
compute.
Octo~\citep{octo2024} (93M) and OpenVLA~\citep{kim2024openvla} (7B) are
open-source alternatives designed for efficient fine-tuning.
$\pi_0$~\citep{black2024pi0} uses a flow-matching action head for
dexterous manipulation.
None of these models incorporate language-encoded action or consequence
feedback at inference time.

\paragraph{Language as an Interface for RL.}
Grounding RL signals in language was explored by
ELLM~\citep{du2023ellm}, which uses an LLM to generate shaped rewards
from environment observations.
SayCan~\citep{ahn2022saycan} uses value functions to score language-level
plans.
Our work differs: we encode action outcomes \emph{directly as CLIP tokens}
rather than using language for planning or reward shaping.

\paragraph{Sequence Models for RL.}
The Decision Transformer~\citep{chen2021decision} treats offline RL as
conditional sequence prediction over (return-to-go, state, action) tokens.
Gato~\citep{reed2022gato} multi-task sequences interleave tokens across
modalities.
Both encode history numerically; we encode it as language.

\paragraph{Contrastive Alignment.}
CLIP~\citep{radford2021clip} trains shared vision-language embeddings via
InfoNCE~\citep{oord2018infonce}.
We repurpose InfoNCE as an \emph{auxiliary} loss that pulls successful
action-consequence pairs toward the instruction embedding, not as a
pretraining objective.
ViLT~\citep{kim2021vilt} introduced modality-type embeddings to distinguish
token sources; we extend this to five modalities.

\section{VLLA Architecture}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Overview}

\modelname\ processes five input streams at each timestep $t$:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Vision}: $T=3$ RGB frames $\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times224\times224}$
  \item \textbf{Instruction}: fixed natural-language goal tokens (CLIP BPE, 77 ids)
  \item \textbf{Action Language} (feedback): previous action index $a_{t-1}$
  \item \textbf{Consequence Language} (feedback, \textbf{new}): reward $r_{t-1}$
        and distance delta $\Delta d_{t-1}$
  \item \textbf{History}: rolling window
        $\mathcal{H}_t=\{(a_{t-H},r_{t-H}),\ldots,(a_{t-1},r_{t-1})\}$
\end{enumerate}

Streams 3, 4, and 5 constitute the \textbf{feedback loop}:
outputs from timestep $t$ become inputs at timestep $t+1$.
The full pipeline is shown in
\cref{fig:arch_2d} (flat pipeline view) and
\cref{fig:arch_3d} (isometric view emphasising stream separation).

\subsection{Frozen CLIP Backbone}

CLIP ViT-B/32~\citep{radford2021clip} is shared across Streams 1--3b and
kept entirely frozen throughout training.
\textbf{Vision} (Stream~1): each $224\times224$ frame is split into $7\times7$
patches, processed by 12 ViT layers, and reduced to a 512-dim global
embedding.
\textbf{Instruction} (Stream~2) and \textbf{Action Language} (Stream~3a)
use the CLIP text encoder on pre-tokenised strings.
\textbf{Consequence Language} (Stream~3b) tokenises dynamically per
forward pass, since the output of \texttt{verbalize\_consequence()} is
runtime-dependent.

Each stream has an independent trainable projection head:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}^{(s)} = \mathrm{RMSNorm}\!\left(W^{(s)}\,\mathbf{e}^{(s)}_\text{CLIP}\right),
  \quad W^{(s)}\in\mathbb{R}^{256\times512}, \quad s\in\{1,2,3a,3b\}
\end{equation}
Independent projections allow each stream to occupy a specialised region
of the 256-dim fusion space without interfering with other streams.

\subsection{Action Language Encoder (Stream 3a)}

For each action index $a\in\{0,\ldots,N\}$, a fixed vocabulary maps $a$ to
a natural-language description (\emph{e.g.}\ ``\emph{I moved the gripper left}'').
The vocabulary is pre-tokenised once and cached; encoding is a single
\texttt{clip.encode\_text} call:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}_{3a} = \mathrm{CLIP}_\text{text}\!\left(\mathrm{vocab}[a_{t-1}]\right)
                    \in\mathbb{R}^{512}
\end{equation}
A learned reward gate scales the projected token by the previous reward:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{3a} = \sigma\!\left(\mathrm{MLP}(r_{t-1})\right)
                    \cdot \mathrm{RMSNorm}(W^{(3a)}\,\mathbf{e}_{3a})
  \label{eq:reward_gate}
\end{equation}
The gate $\sigma\!\left(\cdot\right)\in(0,1)$ attenuates action tokens for
failed steps, letting the model weight successful history more heavily.

\subsection{Consequence Language Encoder (Stream 3b)}
\label{sec:consequence}

The consequence encoder translates the \emph{observed outcome} of the
previous action into language at runtime.

\paragraph{Verbalization function.}
\begin{equation}
  c_{t-1} = \texttt{verbalize\_consequence}(r_{t-1},\,\Delta d_{t-1})
\end{equation}
\begin{align*}
  \Delta d_{t-1} < -0.05 &\;\Rightarrow\; \text{``As a result, I got closer to the goal''} \\
  \Delta d_{t-1} >  0.05 &\;\Rightarrow\; \text{``As a result, I moved farther from the goal''} \\
  |{\Delta d_{t-1}}| \leq 0.05 &\;\Rightarrow\; \text{``My position relative to the goal barely changed''} \\
  r_{t-1} > 0.5 &\;\Rightarrow\; \text{``The action was successful and I received a positive reward''} \\
  r_{t-1} < -0.05 &\;\Rightarrow\; \text{``The action failed and I received a penalty''}
\end{align*}
The function checks $\Delta d$ first (more informative) and falls back to
reward magnitude.
The resulting string is CLIP-tokenised and encoded per forward pass:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{3b} = \mathrm{RMSNorm}\!\left(W^{(3b)}\,
    \mathrm{CLIP}_\text{text}\!\left(c_{t-1}\right)\right)
  \in\mathbb{R}^{256}
  \label{eq:consequence}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Why a separate stream from Stream~3a?}
Stream~3a encodes \emph{what was attempted}; Stream~3b encodes
\emph{what happened as a result}.
These are semantically orthogonal: the same action (``move gripper left'')
can produce different consequences depending on the scene.
Independent projection weights $W^{(3b)} \neq W^{(3a)}$ allow the two
streams to specialise in the fusion space.

\subsection{Action-Reward History Encoder (Stream 4)}

For history step $i\in\{t-H,\ldots,t-1\}$:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{a}_i &= \mathrm{Embedding}(a_i;\;W_a)\in\mathbb{R}^{256}
                  \label{eq:action_emb}\\
  \mathbf{r}_i &= \mathrm{Linear}(r_i;\;W_r)\in\mathbb{R}^{256}
                  \label{eq:reward_proj}\\
  \mathbf{h}_i &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
                    \mathrm{Linear}([\mathbf{a}_i\,\|\,\mathbf{r}_i])
                    + \mathrm{SinPE}(i)
                  \right)
                  \label{eq:hist_token}
\end{align}
The $H=4$ history tokens are then processed by a 2-layer LLaMA-style
transformer before entering the main fusion stack, giving the history
encoder a dedicated capacity to model temporal correlations in the $(a,r)$
sequence.

\subsection{Token Sequence and ViLT Modality Embeddings}
\label{sec:token_seq}

All projected tokens are concatenated:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{X} = [\,
    \underbrace{\mathbf{z}_{2}}_{\text{instr}} \;\|\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{z}_{3a}}_{\text{action}} \;\|\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{z}_{3b}}_{\text{conseq.}} \;\|\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{z}_{1}^{1}\cdots\mathbf{z}_{1}^{T}}_{\text{vision}} \;\|\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{h}_{t-H}\cdots\mathbf{h}_{t-1}}_{\text{history}} \;\|\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{c}}_{\text{CLS}}
  \,] \;\in\mathbb{R}^{(1+1+1+T+H+1)\times256}
  \label{eq:sequence}
\end{equation}
with $T=3$, $H=4$: $S = 1+1+1+3+4+1 = 11$ tokens.

Following ViLT~\citep{kim2021vilt}, each token receives an additional
\emph{modality-type embedding} $\mathbf{m}^{(s)}\in\mathbb{R}^{256}$
(one learnable vector per stream type):
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{cll}
\toprule
Type id & Stream & Token(s) \\
\midrule
0 & Instruction / CLS & $\mathbf{z}_2$,~$\mathbf{c}$ \\
1 & Action language   & $\mathbf{z}_{3a}$ \\
2 & Vision            & $\mathbf{z}_1^1$,~$\mathbf{z}_1^2$,~$\mathbf{z}_1^3$ \\
3 & History           & $\mathbf{h}_{t-4}\cdots\mathbf{h}_{t-1}$ \\
4 & Consequence \textcolor{OrangeNew}{[new]} & $\mathbf{z}_{3b}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{LLaMA Fusion Transformer}
\label{sec:fusion}

A six-layer LLaMA-style Transformer fuses the token sequence under a
causal mask so that CLS at position~10 attends to all preceding tokens:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{X}^{(\ell+1)}
  = \mathbf{X}^{(\ell)}
  + \mathrm{MHA}_\text{RoPE}\!\left(\mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{X}^{(\ell)}),\,\mathbf{M}\right)
  + \mathrm{SwiGLU}\!\left(\mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{X}^{(\ell)})\right)
  \label{eq:llama_block}
\end{equation}
Key design choices vs.\ a standard Transformer:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{RMSNorm}~\citep{zhang2019rmsnorm} instead of LayerNorm:
        no mean centering, fewer parameters, more numerically stable.
  \item \textbf{RoPE}~\citep{su2021rope} applied to Q and K:
        relative positional information preserved in attention dot-products;
        generalises to sequence lengths unseen during training.
  \item \textbf{SwiGLU}~\citep{shazeer2020swiglu}:
        $\text{SwiGLU}(x) = \text{SiLU}(W_1 x)\otimes W_2 x$,
        empirically outperforms GELU MLP with the same parameter count.
  \item \textbf{No bias} in projection layers (LLaMA convention).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Action Head}

The CLS output $\mathbf{c}'\in\mathbb{R}^{256}$ is decoded by a 3-layer MLP:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{y}} = W_3\,\mathrm{SiLU}(W_2\,\mathrm{SiLU}(W_1\,\mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{c}'))),
  \quad \hat{\mathbf{y}}\in\mathbb{R}^N
  \label{eq:head}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reward-Weighted InfoNCE Alignment Loss}
\label{sec:alignment}

During training an auxiliary contrastive loss is applied in CLIP's 512-dim
space (before projection), pulling successful action/consequence embeddings
toward the instruction embedding:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{align}
  = \frac{1}{2}\Bigl[
    \mathrm{InfoNCE}(\mathbf{e}_2,\,\mathbf{e}_{3a},\,r)
    +
    \mathrm{InfoNCE}(\mathbf{e}_2,\,\mathbf{e}_{3b},\,r)
  \Bigr]
  \label{eq:alignment}
\end{equation}
where the reward-weighted InfoNCE for a batch of $B$ samples is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{InfoNCE}(\mathbf{e}_q,\,\mathbf{e}_k,\,r)
  = -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}
    r_i \cdot
    \log\frac{\exp(\mathbf{e}^{(i)}_q\cdot\mathbf{e}^{(i)}_k/\tau)}
             {\sum_{j=1}^{B}\exp(\mathbf{e}^{(i)}_q\cdot\mathbf{e}^{(j)}_k/\tau)}
  \label{eq:infonce}
\end{equation}
The reward weighting ensures that only \emph{successful} steps contribute
to alignment: failed actions should \emph{not} be brought close to the
instruction embedding (they represent what \emph{not} to do).

\section{Training}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Phase 1 — Behavioural Cloning}

Cross-entropy loss with label smoothing $\epsilon=0.05$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{BC}
  = -(1-\epsilon)\log\hat{y}_{a^*}
    - \frac{\epsilon}{N}\sum_{a=0}^{N-1}\log\hat{y}_a
  \label{eq:bc_loss}
\end{equation}
During SFT, $r_{t-1}$ and $\Delta d_{t-1}$ are approximated from
demonstration success labels; the consequence stream receives a neutral
token for failed lookups.

\subsection{Phase 2 — Online REINFORCE with KL Regularisation}

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{RL}
  = \underbrace{-\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log\pi_\theta(a_t)\cdot\hat{G}_t\right]}_{\text{REINFORCE}}
  + \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\!\left[(V(s_t)-G_t)^2\right]}_{\text{value baseline}}
  - \underbrace{0.01\,\mathcal{H}[\pi_\theta]}_{\text{entropy}}
  + \underbrace{0.1\,D_\text{KL}(\pi_\theta\,\|\,\pi_\text{BC})}_{\text{no forgetting}}
  \label{eq:rl_loss}
\end{equation}

The consequence and action streams are fully active during RL: the
environment provides real rewards and the env wrapper extracts
\texttt{dist\_delta} from end-effector position (MetaWorld) or Manhattan
distance to goal object (BabyAI).

\paragraph{Total training loss.}
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{RL} + \lambda_\text{align}\,\mathcal{L}_\text{align},
  \quad \lambda_\text{align}=0.1
  \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Environments}

\paragraph{BabyAI / MiniGrid~\citep{minigrid}.}
Grid-world environments with procedurally generated language instructions
(``\emph{go to the red ball}'') and a 7-action discrete space.
\texttt{dist\_delta} is the change in Manhattan distance from the agent to
the target object.
Cheap to run; good for ablations and feedback loop validation.

\paragraph{Meta-World~\citep{yu2020metaworld}.}
50 tabletop manipulation tasks with a 4-DoF continuous action space
(discretised to $N$ bins via a fixed codebook).
\texttt{dist\_delta} = change in end-effector–to–goal Euclidean distance.
The primary benchmark for real-task performance.

\subsection{Baselines}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Memoryless VLA} ($H=0$, no feedback): identical architecture
        with all feedback streams ablated.
  \item \textbf{Numerical history only} (Ablation A): only Stream~4,
        no language feedback.
  \item \textbf{Action token only} (Ablation F): Stream~3a only,
        no consequence token.
  \item \textbf{\modelname\ (ours)}: all five streams.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \modelname, a five-stream multimodal robot policy that encodes
both \emph{what was attempted} and \emph{what happened as a result} as
language tokens using a shared frozen CLIP encoder.
The dual language feedback mechanism is lightweight (adds $\approx$0.4M
trainable parameters), requires no additional pretraining, and integrates
seamlessly with the existing CLIP semantic space.
The reward-weighted InfoNCE auxiliary loss further aligns the feedback
tokens with the task instruction, teaching the model which past actions
are worth repeating.
Together with a LLaMA-style fusion backbone and ViLT modality-type
embeddings, \modelname\ enables adaptive, memory-informed robot decision-making
on consumer-grade hardware.

%% ============================================================================
\section*{Limitations}

\textbf{Discrete action spaces only.}
The action head is a classifier; continuous control would require tokenised
or regression output.

\textbf{Verbalization granularity.}
\texttt{verbalize\_consequence()} maps continuous signals to a small set of
template strings.
A learned or generative verbalizer could capture finer-grained outcome
distinctions.

\textbf{Single-step consequence.}
Only the most recent $(r_{t-1}, \Delta d_{t-1})$ is verbalized.
Multi-step consequence descriptions (summarising the last $H$ steps) are a
natural extension.

\textbf{Simulated environments only.}
The \texttt{RealEnv} stub is unvalidated on physical hardware.
Domain randomisation and sensor-noise modelling are required for
sim-to-real transfer.

\textbf{Results pending.}
The experimental tables contain placeholder results pending completion of
multi-seed training runs.

%% ============================================================================
\section*{Broader Impact}

History-conditioned policies reduce human re-intervention for failed robot
actions, improving autonomy in assistive and industrial settings.
The 4.2M-trainable-parameter design democratises robot learning by enabling
training without accelerator clusters.
On the negative side, more autonomous robots could displace workers.
The consequence language feedback mechanism could theoretically be
manipulated by adversarially crafted reward signals, though this requires
physical access to the reward channel and is mitigated by the reward gate
clamping extreme values.

\begin{table}[h]

\label{tab:alternatives}
\small
\begin{tabular}{L{4cm}L{8.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Alternative} & \textbf{Reason for rejection / limitation} \\
\midrule
Raw numerical $(a, r)$ token &
  Bypasses CLIP's semantic structure. Requires the fusion transformer to
  learn action semantics from scratch. \\
One-hot action embedding &
  No semantic similarity between nearby actions. Hard to generalise to new
  action vocabularies. \\
LSTM over history &
  Implicit, non-inspectable memory. Gradient vanishing for long sequences.
  Cannot inspect which past step influenced the decision. \\
Single feedback token (action only) &
  Action and consequence are semantically orthogonal; one token conflates
  them. Ablation~F quantifies this. \\
Learned verbalizer (fine-tuned LLM) &
  Adds $>$1B parameters, requires additional pretraining, not compatible
  with frozen CLIP. \\
